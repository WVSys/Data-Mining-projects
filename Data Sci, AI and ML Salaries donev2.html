Problem¶

Job hunting is stressful for anyone entering the workforce. Candidates often struggle to determine the fair market value of their skills, while employers are in a stronger position with more information about prevailing salaries. This imbalance can leave job seekers at a disadvantage.

For this project, I focus specifically on Computer Science and Data Science graduates entering the job market. My aim is to analyze global salary data for Computer Science and Data Science related roles, with particular attention to entry-level positions. By examining salary patterns across job titles, I hope to highlight what recent graduates can realistically expect as they begin their careers and reduce some of the uncertainty that comes with negotiating a first job offer.

Red Flags¶

Technical Writer listed with a salary of $500,000 was the canary in the coal mine that suggested something was wrong. To investigate further, I pulled the top 30 salaries by job title, and the results confirmed my suspicions: analysts, engineers, and other roles had reported salaries far beyond realistic market values (some approaching 800k).

These outliers are highly unlikely to be accurate. They distort the dataset, making it impossible to draw reliable insights.

Next step: remove these implausible entries. I chose to drop all salaries above $300,000, which preserves legitimate high-paying executive roles while discarding extreme and likely erroneous values.

Removing the Skew¶

After reviewing the dataset, I identified several unrealistic salary values (e.g., entry-level roles exceeding $500k). To address this, I set $300k as a reasonable upper limit and removed any entries above that threshold.

When comparing before and after, the data distribution is much tighter. Most salaries now fall between $100k and $200k, which aligns more closely with expectations.

However, this view still represents the entire dataset across all experience levels.

Therefore, I chose to first generate a list of flagged titles, review them manually, and then apply a refined filter. This ensures that I only remove mislabeled rows (e.g., “Entry-level Director” or “Entry-level Engineering Manager”) while preserving legitimate roles.

Clean point¶

At this stage, I separated leadership roles (titles containing words like Manager, Director, Head, Lead, etc.) from non-leadership roles. The reasoning is that some job titles in the dataset are mislabeled as entry level (EN) even though they are clearly management positions. Including those would artificially inflate entry-level salary estimates.

By splitting these groups, I can better focus on the roles that are realistic for new graduates, while still keeping transparency about what data was excluded. This step is part of the preprocessing process and ensures that the analysis reflects a more accurate picture of true entry-level opportunities.

non_leadership_roles = salaries_clean[~mask]

On with the Show¶

Here I plot two graphs to better understand the distribution of entry-level salaries.

Boxplot: This shows the spread and quartiles. The median is around 86k, with most of the data falling between 60k and 120k. At first glance, one might assume that 86k is the “expected” salary.

Histogram: This offers clearer insight into where salaries actually cluster. The highest frequency is in the 90k–100k range, showing that many entry-level roles concentrate here rather than evenly around the median.

Together, these two views highlight the difference between a statistical middle point (median) and the practical reality of where most offers land.

Grain of Salt¶

The dataset is large, but not comprehensive. Many assumptions had to be made and several anomalies corrected, so confidence in the results is not absolute. Still, it provides a meaningful snapshot of the job market.

This analysis focuses specifically on entry-level salaries and posting frequency. A fuller picture would require additional context, such as job descriptions, company size, industry, and cost-of-living adjustments. Without these factors, the data can be misinterpreted. For instance, graduates outside the U.S. could be misled by the heavy U.S. skew and expect salaries that don’t match their local markets. Additionally because the analysis is not broken down by specific job title, salary expectations may vary significantly across roles. What this project offers is not a definitive answer, but a broad directional view of what entry-level compensation may look like.

This is a very broad look as this dataset spans 5 years worth of data and I do not breakdown jobs specifically. One can be misled to believe that alot of jobs offer the median range when it is only a certain type that offers that. This is intended to get a feel only.

Closing Time¶

So what does all of this mean for a new Computer Science/Data Science graduate in 2025? The data suggests a median entry-level salary of about $86k, but the clustering of salaries between $90k and $100k implies that aiming higher than the median is realistic and even advisable.

While this dataset is not perfect, it still equips graduates with a stronger sense of what to expect as they enter the job market. More importantly, it gives them leverage in negotiations — a clearer idea of the going rate for their skills and the confidence to push for compensation that reflects the true market.
